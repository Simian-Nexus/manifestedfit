## VOICE 1 — Grounded & Practical
**Suggested columnist:** *Dana Cole* — "cuts the fluff, keeps the warmth."

# The 5-Minute Morning Reset That Actually Sticks

Most mornings don't fall apart because you're lazy. They fall apart because you hand the day away before you're even upright — to your phone, your inbox, the pile of things you're behind on. By the time the coffee's poured, your body is already running on someone else's agenda.

Here's the fix. It takes five minutes and zero willpower.

[VIDEO EMBED]

## Why the first five minutes decide the rest

When you wake up, your body is choosing a setting: calm and clear, or braced and scattered. Grab your phone first thing and you've picked braced — every notification is a tiny stress hit before you've sat up.

People treat manifestation like it's all in your head. It isn't. Your body gets a vote. It's a lot easier to hold a steady intention when your nervous system is actually steady. So we do it in that order: settle the body, then set the intention.

## The reset, step by step

No equipment. No app. You can do this on the edge of your bed.

**1. Breathe (90 seconds).** Five slow breaths, exhale longer than the inhale — in for four, out for six. The long exhale is the part that signals "you're safe." Highest-leverage thing you'll do all morning. Costs you 90 seconds.

**2. Set one intention (60 seconds).** Not a to-do list — a tone. "How do I want to feel, and what's the one thing that matters today?" Say it plainly: *I move through today calm and focused.* One beats ten.

**3. Move gently (2 minutes).** Reach up, lengthen your spine, roll your shoulders, a couple of easy side bends. That's it. You're telling your body "we're on" without jolting it awake.

**4. Anchor it (30 seconds).** Tie the reset to something you already do — filling the kettle, feeding the dog. That link is what makes it automatic instead of optional.

## Why this beats a "stricter" routine

Strict routines die because they run on willpower, and willpower is broke at 6am. This one runs on ease. It's short enough that skipping it feels dumber than doing it. Small win, repeated, compounds. That's the whole trick.
